The Value of Experience — Good Will Hunting and What it Means to ‘Know’

philosophy
On Frank Jackson’s qualia, Mary’s Room, and what a park bench scene in Good Will Hunting tells us about the limits of knowledge.
Published

September 6, 2021

After a recent re-watch of ‘Good Will Hunting’ — one of my favourite films — I was again struck by the remarkable conversation between troubled young janitor Will Hunting (Matt Damon), an initially unrecognised genius, and his therapist, Sean (Robin Williams), whom Will is forced to see as part of a deal to stay out of prison. In this specific scene, which takes place on a park bench overlooking a pond, Sean verbally reflects on Will’s intellectual prowess — his ability to critically process and recall staggering amounts of information seemingly without any difficulty — but also his tendency to use this ability to avoid opening up emotionally, thereby hurting himself and those close to him. This reflection follows from their previous meeting, in which Will arrogantly assumes to know Sean after one conversation and an analysis of a painting in the latter’s office — a personal affront that led to the therapist losing his temper.

Of course, the thought that one cannot truly know another is not unintuitive or particularly strange to us. It is often the case that there are substantial differences even in the way we experience the exact same phenomenon. For instance, think back to 2015 and the fervent debates surrounding the colour of the accursed dress pictured here (I know, simpler times). In this way, even if Will had, in fact, experienced the same things that Sean has, he would still not have been able to know Sean.

Regardless of this, Sean’s point that the experience of something adds to the knowledge of that thing seems to me a clear homage to philosopher Frank Jackson’s famous 1982 article “Epiphenomenal Qualia”. In this (scary-sounding) article, Jackson introduces a thought experiment colloquially known as Mary’s Room — a popular thought experiment in undergraduate philosophy of mind courses, where I had the privilege of encountering it first.

The experiment asks that the reader imagine a brilliant neuroscientist, Mary (or a certain Will Hunting, for that matter), to exist permanently in a room devoid of colours other than black and white. In this room, Mary has access to say, a black and white version of the internet. Due to being a highly talented scientist and having access to all of the resources necessary for obtaining knowledge, Mary knows all there is to know of the accounts, descriptions and empirical evidence on colour. She knows which frequencies and intensities are associated with each colour in the visible spectrum, knows how the brain biologically processes these colours, and knows how other people have attempted to describe the experience of colour. However, and crucially, one must also imagine that Mary has never actually perceived colour herself. All she sees, and ever has seen, has been in black and white. The primary question the experiment requires us to ponder on is this: will Mary gain any new knowledge when she goes outside of this room; when she physically experiences seeing in colour for the first time?

According to Jackson, the answer to this question provides evidence either for or against the existence of qualia — the subjective and purely qualitative properties of experiences. If Mary learns something new about sunsets as soon as she looks at that first rust-coloured sunset, then it would be proof that experiencing something adds to the knowledge of that thing. This, in turn, would mean the falsity of physicalism — the metaphysical belief that everything in the world, all of reality, is physical in essence, and that physical explanations of things like consciousness and our mental states fully encompass the knowledge we can gather about these phenomena. Although this may sound intuitive to many of us, it is still a highly contested argument, one that I invite you to look into yourself.

Jackson’s argument encapsulates one of these contestations. He believes there is something other than the physical that can add value to what we consider knowledge — these subjective experiences. Even though Mary may know everything about the science of colour perception, she cannot know what the experience of blue is like if she has never seen blue, and therefore does not know everything there is to know about blue. Jackson contends that she has learned something new via this non-physical experience, and hence, that complete knowledge of something must require the subjective experience of that thing.

Let us reconsider once again the scene from Good Will Hunting. It seems that Sean, our therapist, is a proponent of the existence of qualia. He claims that because Will has never experienced being in the Sistine Chapel, has never smelled it or looked upon Michelangelo’s ceiling, he therefore can never know it in its entirety, regardless of whether he is a genius or not. Like Mary from the experiment, Will is living in a metaphorically ‘colourless’ world if he believes that his physical knowledge encapsulates the total amount of knowledge on any subject. Sean hopes that making this point proves to Will that his intellectual ability is not all that encompasses him, thereby encouraging him to open up and be honest about his actual lived-experience. This is not just interesting from the perspective of the philosophical value of experience, but naturally also from a psychological one. Regardless, I would highly recommend watching the scene, or better yet, the film itself, to fully appreciate the subtleties underlying many of the excellent dialogues in it.
