Football, Absurdity and Camus

philosophy
On the absurdity of football, Albert Camus, and why Sisyphus might have been kicking a ball around all along.
Published

October 25, 2020

Recently — after a long injury-induced hiatus — I joined a group of friends for a friendly Friday afternoon game of football. After the session, and while I was nursing my aching old body back to health, I was reminded of the often undervalued role that the sport has had on my life. I’ve enjoyed many carefree moments whilst chasing after a ball, happily engaged in this activity. Similarly, I’ve spent countless hours staring at a television screen whilst 22 other blokes pass a round object about (although, as an Arsenal fan, these hours were often not particularly happy). This seems puzzling. What could be so fascinating about a bunch of people running around on a rectangular field of grass for 90 minutes whilst trying to kick an inflated sphere over some arbitrary chalked line, and then go absolutely berserk when achieving this? It seems decidedly devoid of any importance — absurd even. The absurdity is compounded when one considers the proportion of people who have dedicated their entire lives to this activity.

On the occasions that I’ve had to explain why football captures the imagination the way it does, I’ve always found it practically impossible. How does one explain the beauty of a top-corner volley, or the feeling of being crunched in a shocking studs-up tackle, or the absolute despondence of getting an injury and missing out on something that brings you happiness? It always felt like you had to get it. That’s why I don’t even try to explain why these days, except for asserting the ole “because I like it” conversation-ender. Luckily, however, in my post-game reflection, I recalled one who might help in explaining how to deal with the absurdity of football. One who was, coincidentally, also a fan of the beautiful game.

Naturally, our guide is Albert Camus, the famous existentialist philosopher (although he would object to both these descriptors). Camus had an interesting relationship with existence, and with football. Born in Algiers into relative poverty, he found consolation in the escapism provided by the footballing field, going on to represent the junior team of Racing Universitaire d’Alger (RUA) as a goalkeeper. The quote below — as well as this interview discussing his Nobel Prize, held at a live football match — is evidence of the importance that football had in shaping his thought.

Perhaps, in addition to influencing his view on morality, the absurdity of the game also influenced Camus’s position on existence. He developed his thought to speak to the almost-central existentialist question — and probably the one most associated with the field of philosophy — on the meaning of existence: why are we even here? What does it mean to exist? Camus, in contrast to many others before him, posited that there can be no answer to this question. We shouldn’t look for any answers about purpose from our collective human exploits, or indeed from any aspect of the universe. Rather, the world does not follow the rational structure we expect it to. It is in this — the paradox that arises from our inevitable striving for meaning in the face of an ultimately unsympathetic and meaningless world — that Camus identifies the absurd.

“The absurd is born of this confrontation between the human need and the unreasonable silence of the world” — The Myth of Sisyphus

Camus’s understanding of absurdity is illustrated by an example from Greek mythology, the myth of Sisyphus. Without going into too much detail: Sisyphus was condemned by Zeus to strenuously roll a boulder to the top of a mountain, only to watch it roll back down again. Time and again. Forever. This seems quite tragic (not to mention tiring). However, Sisyphus knows what fate has given him: an eternity of toil and suffering with no clear goal or purpose. He knows that he is engaging in something ridiculous, something absurd, and that he cannot escape this bondage. It is in this self-knowledge where Camus sees an antidote to the absurd. Sisyphus, by accepting his fate and becoming conscious of it, becomes master over it. The acceptance of his fate affords him the possibility to shape his perception of it, and his perception can be one where life is full of vitality despite the constraints that his fate imposes on him. When the spectre of meaninglessness looks us in the eyes, the only remaining counter is to completely and unfailingly accept its gaze (and then invite him for a friendly game of footie).

Perhaps, we should accept that football — and indeed all other activities in life — is ultimately absurd; there is no purpose in kicking a round piece of leather around on a field of grass, and attempting to explain why it is meaningful is itself a ludicrous exercise. If, like Sisyphus, we acknowledge this meaninglessness and accept it, maybe we can create its importance anew. Maybe it is even possible to reimagine Sisyphus’s predicament. Instead of rolling a boulder up a hill, perhaps Sisyphus was kicking a ball about on a field all along, taking a shot at goal, missing, and then trudging after it. Maybe, in this situation, we should imagine that Sisyphus is as happy as I — and countless others — have been, doing this exact thing.
